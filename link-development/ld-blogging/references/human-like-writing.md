# Human-Like Writing Reference

This file defines the writing style rules for all blog and article content at Link Development. Apply every principle below when drafting or rewriting content. The goal is copy that reads like a knowledgeable person wrote it — not a content generator.

---

## The Core Problem to Solve

AI-generated writing has a recognisable fingerprint: passive voice, hedging qualifiers, excessive structure, corporate buzzwords, and a tone that lectures rather than converses. These rules exist to strip all of that out and replace it with writing that feels direct, honest, and human.

---

## Writing Mechanics

**Use active voice.** The subject does the thing — it doesn't have things done to it. "Management cancelled the meeting" is cleaner and more direct than "the meeting was cancelled by management." Active voice creates forward momentum in a sentence.

**Address the reader directly.** Use "you" and "your" throughout. Writing that speaks to someone lands differently than writing that describes things in the abstract.

**Cut straight to the point.** Open with substance, not setup. No "In today's fast-paced digital landscape..." No "It is important to consider the following..." Get to what you're actually saying in the first sentence.

**Choose the simpler word every time.** "Fix" beats "remediate." "Use" beats "leverage." "Problem" beats "challenge." "Start" beats "initiate." If a shorter or more common word exists, use it.

**Remove filler and hedging.** Strike phrases like "it's worth noting that," "it's important to understand," "let's explore," "in conclusion," and "needless to say." These phrases exist to pad — not to communicate. Cut them.

**No dashes of any kind.** Avoid hyphens used as pauses, en dashes, and em dashes. If you're tempted to use a dash, either restructure the sentence or use a period. This applies to inline dashes that interrupt sentences, not to compound adjectives where a hyphen is grammatically required.

**No emojis, asterisks for emphasis, or decorative characters** unless Khaled specifically requests them. Let the words carry the weight.

**Vary sentence length deliberately.** Short sentences punch. Longer sentences, when they carry more complex ideas, can earn their length — but they should never ramble without purpose. Mix both throughout and you get natural rhythm. Monotone sentence length is one of the clearest signals of AI-generated text.

---

## Tone & Style

**Write like you're talking to someone, not presenting at them.** Conversational doesn't mean casual — it means direct, warm, and clear. A professional who knows their subject well doesn't speak in corporate abstractions. They explain things clearly and with confidence.

**Be honest about limitations and problems.** "This approach has a real drawback" builds more trust than "this approach presents certain considerations." Readers notice when you're hedging. Acknowledging difficulty makes the rest of what you say more credible.

**No marketing hype.** "Cutting-edge solution delivers unparalleled results" means nothing to a reader. "Our tool helps teams track project expenses in real time" means something. Concrete, specific language always beats vague promotional language.

**Use definitive statements.** "This improves results" is stronger than "this may potentially improve results." Own the claim or don't make it.

**Avoid AI clichés without exception.** Never write "let's dive in," "game-changer," "unlock potential," "revolutionise," "in this article, we will explore," or "stay ahead of the curve." These phrases have been drained of meaning through overuse.

---

## Authenticity Markers

**Start sentences with conjunctions when it feels natural.** "And," "But," and "So" at the start of a sentence are fine in conversational writing. They create flow. Formal grammar rules against this don't apply here.

**Use contractions.** "Don't," "we're," "that's," "it's," "you'll" — these are how people actually talk. Avoiding contractions makes writing feel stiff and formal in a way that creates distance from the reader.

**Ask rhetorical questions when they add connection.** "Sound familiar?" or "What does that actually mean in practice?" can create a sense of dialogue and invite the reader to think alongside you. Don't overdo it — one or two per article is enough.

**Make abstract ideas concrete with analogies and examples.** If you're writing about a complex concept, find the real-world equivalent. "Think of it like..." or "For example, imagine..." helps readers anchor to something they already understand.

**Write for someone specific, not everyone.** Before drafting, picture the actual reader — an IT director in a government department, a project manager at a regional bank, a CIO evaluating an ERP upgrade. Write for that person. Writing for "everyone" produces content that resonates with no one.

**Show empathy for the reader's situation.** Acknowledge the difficulty of what they're dealing with before offering solutions. Readers feel understood when a piece starts by accurately describing their problem before trying to solve it.

---

## Paragraph & Structure Rules

**Mix paragraph lengths.** A one-sentence paragraph lands like a punch. A longer paragraph earns its space by developing an idea fully. Varying both creates the visual and mental rhythm that keeps readers moving through the piece.

**One idea per paragraph.** The moment a paragraph tries to carry two separate points, it loses clarity. Break them up.

**No unnecessary headers in short pieces.** Headers are for navigation — they help readers scan long documents. In articles under 1,000 words, heavy use of headers makes the piece feel like a bullet-point list rather than writing.

---

## Quality Check

Before delivering a draft, read it out loud in your head. If it sounds like something you would actually say to a knowledgeable colleague, it passes. If it sounds like a whitepaper, a press release, or a landing page — rewrite it.

Ask: Is every sentence earning its place? Does the opening pull the reader in immediately? Is there any sentence that could be cut without losing meaning? Cut it.
